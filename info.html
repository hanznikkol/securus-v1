Panic Attack versus Anxiety Attack

Although a panic attack and anxiety attack may be similar in some aspects, they still have their own differences. These differences makes them somehow easier to distinguish by people, especially the professionals.

The following are
the differences of the two:

Anxiety Attack

- not a diagnosable condition
- can have a specific trigger, such as an exam, workplace issues, a health issue, or a relationship problem
- usually develops gradually when a person feels anxious
- involves physical symptoms, such as a racing heart or knot in the stomach

Panic Attack

- a diagnosable condition
- come on suddenly and involve intense and often overwhelming fear
- often come unexpected or expected
- involves physical symptoms and feelings of terror so intense that the person fears a total loss of control or imminent death
- often occurs suddenly and unexpectedly and last between a few minutes and an hour, although the negative impact may continue

Panic Attack

Individuals with panic disorder have abrupt and repeated attacks of dread that keep going for a few minutes or more. These are called panic attacks. Panic attacks are characterized by a fear of disaster or of losing control even when there is no real danger. A person may also have a strong physical reaction during a panic attack. It may feel like having a heart attack. Panic attacks can occur at any time, and many people with panic disorder worry about and dread the possibility of having another attack.


A person with panic disorder may become discouraged and feel ashamed because he or she cannot carry out normal routines like going to school or work, going to the grocery store, or driving.


Panic disorder often begins in the late teens or early adulthood. More women than men have panic disorder. But not everyone who experiences panic attacks will develop panic disorder.

Symptoms

When people experience panic attacks, they often have sudden and repeated panic attacks of overwhelming anxiety and fear, they have a feeling of being out of control, or a fear of death or impending doom, they worry intensely about when the next panic attack will happen, and they have the fear or avoidance of places where panic attacks have occurred in the past. The following are the physical symptoms of panic attacks:

Shortness of
breathe

Sweating

Hypertension

Dizziness

Rapid
Heartbeat

Trembling

Causes

Psychological causes:

  • - Chronic stress
  • - An existing mental health condition
  • - Phobias
  • - Lack of assertiveness
  • - Low self-esteem

Pharmacological causes:

  • - Alcohol, drugs or medication withdrawal
  • - Medication side effects
  • - Excessive caffeine intake

Environmental causes:

  • - Chronic physical illnesses
  • - Experiencing a significant personal loss
  • - Significant life changes
  • - ‘Maintaining’ behaviour such as avoiding panic-provoking situations or environments, anxious/negative self-talk, dysfunctional beliefs, and withheld feelings

Treatment

  • - Psychotherapy
  • - Medication (Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors / SSRIs, Serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors / SNRIs, Beta-blockers, Benzodiazepines)

Prevention

  • - Use muscle relaxation techniques.
  • - Take medication.
  • - Try for a balanced diet.
  • - Avoid smoking, caffeine, drinking alcohol, and illegal drugs.
  • - Practice indfulness and meditation
  • - Exercise regularly. It’s a proven stress-buster.
  • - Do breathing exercises.
  • - Get enough restful sleep.
  • - Lastly know your trigger and learn to avoid those things that can trigger your attacks.

Anxiety Attack

Anxiety attacks are episodes of intense panic or fear. Anxiety attacks usually occur suddenly and without warning. Sometimes there’s a specific trigger, like public speaking, pressure in work and school, and family problems.


“Anxiety attack” is not a formal, clinical term. It is not a diagnosable condition and is not a recognized medical condition as defined by the DSM-V. It is a term people with anxiety use to describe very intense or extended periods of anxiety. Anxiety attacks do not necessarily indicate that a person has an anxiety disorder. However, anxiety attacks are still not supposed to be taken lightly.


The symptoms of anxiety attacks are more severe than the simple feeling of anxiety but less severe than a panic attack.


Symptoms

People who experience anxiety attacks often have the surge of overwhelming panic, feeling of losing control or going crazy, and feeling detached or unreal. Aside from thosem the following are the physical symptoms of anxiety attacks:

Trembling

Causes

  • - Pressure
  • - Family or relationships problems
  • - Changing life situations
  • - Upsetting or stressful health issues
  • - Negative thinking, especially when upset or frustrated

Treatment

  • - Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT)
  • - Medications, such as some types of antidepressants
  • - Support groups for people with specific conditions
  • - Seeking professional help

Prevention

  • - Connect with others
  • - Manage stress
  • - Practice relaxation techniques
  • - Exercise regularly
  • - Get enough sleep
  • - Know the signs and your triggers
